## Design Context

### Users
Vocational high school students (직업계고, ages 16-18) in South Korea learning semiconductor manufacturing and entering factory floor training for the first time. They use SafeFactory during classroom safety training sessions, guided by teachers. Their context: learning new technical concepts in Korean, often unfamiliar with professional terminology, potentially anxious about entering industrial workplaces. Teachers are the buyers who assign SafeFactory; students are the daily users. The product is operated by 청년노동자인권센터 (Youth Labor Rights Center), a Seoul-based organization dedicated to protecting young workers' rights and safety.

### Brand Personality
**Three words**: 신뢰적인 (Trustworthy) · 친근한 (Approachable) · 전문적인 (Professional)

**Voice**: Authoritative but never intimidating. Speaks like a caring senior colleague who knows the factory floor, not a textbook or a government notice. Uses 격식 존댓말 (~입니다/~합니다) but keeps sentences short and vocabulary at high-school level. Technical terms always include parenthetical explanations.

**Emotional goals**: Students should feel protected, confident, and capable. The interface should convey "we take your safety seriously" without creating anxiety. Information should feel accessible and actionable, never overwhelming.

### Aesthetic Direction
**Visual identity**: "Industrial Precision" — clean, structured, serious enough for safety-critical content but warm enough for teenagers. Navy (`#1e293b`) as the authority color, Amber (`#f59e0b`) as the warmth/attention accent.

**Typography**:
- Korean body: Noto Sans KR (300-900 weights)
- English headings/logo: Outfit (bold geometric sans)
- Code/data: Space Mono

**Color system**:
- Backgrounds: Cool slate-white spectrum (`#f8f9fb` → `#ffffff`)
- Text: 4-tier hierarchy (slate-900/slate-600/slate-400/slate-300)
- Domains: Each has a distinct accent (cyan for semiconductor, pink for field training, blue for safety, green for MSDS)
- Emergency: Red (`#ef4444`) with pulsing animation for urgent safety content

**Layout**: Max-width 960px. Generous whitespace. 8px border-radius system. Subtle shadows (3-tier). Sticky nav with glass-blur effect.

**Theme**: Light mode only. No dark mode planned.

**Anti-references**:
- Government/institutional websites (딱딱하고 기업적인 느낌) — too cold and formal
- Gamified/playful UIs (장난감 같은 느낌) — safety content demands seriousness
- Information-dense dashboards (정보 과다) — students need clarity, not data overload

### Design Principles

1. **Safety First, Always Visible**: Emergency content gets red treatment, prominent placement, and immediate response. Safety information is never hidden behind extra clicks. When in doubt, over-communicate risk.

2. **Clarity Over Cleverness**: Every screen should be scannable by a 16-year-old seeing it for the first time. Progressive disclosure — show the essential, offer the detail. One primary action per view. Short sentences. Visual hierarchy does the heavy lifting.

3. **Trust Through Precision**: Consistent spacing, aligned elements, careful typography. Small details (proper citation formatting, source attribution, confidence indicators) build trust. Sloppy design undermines credibility for safety-critical content.

4. **Warm Authority**: Navy gives weight and professionalism. Amber gives warmth and human touch. The combination says "we're serious about your safety AND we care about you." Never cold, never frivolous.

5. **Korean-First, Mobile-Aware**: All text, spacing, and line-heights are optimized for Korean typography. Classroom desktop is the primary context, but layouts must work on mobile for occasional field reference. Hangul readability always takes priority over English typographic conventions.
